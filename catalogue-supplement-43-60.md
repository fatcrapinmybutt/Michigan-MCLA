## 43. Pro Se Litigant Rights & Procedural Toolkit

### Constitutional & Statutory Basis for Self-Representation

| Right | Authority | Pinpoint |
|---|---|---|
| Right of self-representation | Mich Const 1963, art I, § 13 | "Every person has the right … to appear and defend in person") |
| Self-representation in court | MCR 2.117(A) | Party may appear in person without attorney |
| Access to courts — fundamental right | *Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 US 371 (1971) | 380–381 (financial barriers cannot deny court access in cases involving fundamental rights) |
| Federal IFP | 28 USC 1915 | § 1915(a)(1) — affidavit of inability to pay |
| State fee waiver | MCR 2.002; MCL 600.2529 | MCR 2.002(A): court shall waive fees upon showing of indigency |

### Lenient Construction of Pro Se Pleadings

- **Federal standard:** *Haines v Kerner*, 404 US 519 (1972) — pro se complaints held to less stringent standards than formal pleadings drafted by lawyers; allegations taken as true.
- **Michigan application:** *Mettler Walloon, LLC v Melrose Twp*, 281 Mich App 184 (2008) — pro se litigants are held to the same procedural rules as attorneys but courts afford leeway on technical defects in pleadings.
- Pro se status does not excuse failure to comply with jurisdictional deadlines; preserve issues for appeal.

### Fee Waivers — State Courts

| Form | Purpose | Authority |
|---|---|---|
| MC 20 | Motion/Affidavit for Waiver of Fees (general) | MCR 2.002; SCAO |
| MC 01 | General motion form | SCAO |
| CC 375 | PPO petition | SCAO |
| CC 376 | PPO (personal protection order) | SCAO |
| CC 380 | PPO — motion to modify/terminate | SCAO |

> *Authority:* MCR 2.002(A) — "A party who cannot afford to pay the costs of an action … may proceed without prepayment of costs." SCAO Form MC 20.

### Appellate Self-Representation

- MCR 7.212 applies equally to pro se litigants; no procedural relaxation for noncompliance with word limits, appendix, or table of contents requirements.
- TrueFiling account required for electronic filing at COA; register at truefiling.com.
- MSC: electronic filing required; see Michigan Courts e-filing portal.
- *Appellate checklist for pro se:* (1) timely claim of appeal or leave application, (2) order transcript (or move for transcript substitute), (3) obtain register of actions, (4) compile appendix, (5) serve all parties.

### No Michigan Vexatious Litigant Statute (Distinction from California)

- Michigan has **no** comprehensive vexatious litigant statute comparable to Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 391 et seq.
- Sanctions available through: MCL 600.2591 (frivolous claims); MCR 1.109(E); MCR 2.625 (costs).
- Constitutional problems with restricting court access for indigent family law litigants: *Boddie v Connecticut*, supra; Mich Const 1963, art I, § 13; First Amendment (right to petition).

## 44. Parenting Time Deprivation — Extended Severance as Constitutional Violation

### Fundamental Rights Framework

| Authority | Proposition | Pinpoint |
|---|---|---|
| *Troxel v Granville*, 530 US 57 (2000) | Parental rights are fundamental liberty interests under the 14th Amendment | 65–66 |
| *Santosky v Kramer*, 455 US 745 (1982) | Clear and convincing evidence required before termination of parental rights | 747–748 |
| *Stanley v Illinois*, 405 US 645 (1972) | Unwed father has due process right to hearing before deprivation of parental rights | 651 |
| *M.L.B. v S.L.J.*, 519 US 102 (1996) | Cannot deny appellate review of TPR based on inability to pay transcript costs | 116 |
| *Mathews v Eldridge*, 424 US 319 (1976) | Three-part balancing test: private interest, risk of erroneous deprivation, government interest | 335 |

### De Facto Termination Doctrine

- **Definition:** Complete severance of parent-child contact for an extended period without formal TPR proceedings may constitute a *de facto* termination of parental rights requiring heightened procedural protections.
- **Rationale:** *Troxel* and *Santosky* establish that informal severing of parental rights through court orders carries the same constitutional weight as formal TPR.
- **Required findings:** Suspension of parenting time must be supported by on-record factual findings — not conclusory assertions.

> *Authority:* *Shulick v Richards*, 273 Mich App 320, 325 (2006) — suspension of parenting time "must be supported by findings" on the record; court cannot summarily suspend without specific articulable basis.

### Michigan Statutory Framework

| Statute | Provision |
|---|---|
| MCL 722.27a | Parenting time is the child's right; court shall order parenting time unless prohibited by a specific finding |
| MCL 722.27a(7) | Remedies available if parent wrongfully denies court-ordered parenting time: make-up time, attorney fees, contempt |
| MCL 722.27a(1) | Parenting time with both parents is presumed in child's best interests |
| MCL 722.23 | Best-interest factors — court must make written findings |

### Extended Deprivation Checklist

- [ ] Document precise dates of denied parenting time with source (opposing party, court order, FOC recommendation)
- [ ] Calculate total duration of severance vs. court-ordered time
- [ ] Identify any formal TPR proceedings — if none, argue *de facto* termination doctrine
- [ ] File written motion for parenting time enforcement under MCL 722.27a(7) — preserves record
- [ ] Seek *Mathews v Eldridge* balancing analysis on each motion to extend suspension
- [ ] If federal action: plead 14th Amendment substantive and procedural due process; cite *Troxel*, *Stanley*, *Santosky*

### Supporting Michigan Caselaw

- *Brausch v Brausch*, 283 Mich App 339 (2009) — parenting time modification requires change in circumstances; court must follow statutory framework.
- *Shulick v Richards*, 273 Mich App 320 (2006) — court must make specific findings supporting suspension of parenting time.

## 45. Contempt — Incarceration Defenses (Expanded)

### Constitutional Prerequisites — Civil Contempt Incarceration

| Authority | Rule | Pinpoint |
|---|---|---|
| *Turner v Rogers*, 564 US 431 (2011) | Due process requires procedural safeguards (notice of importance of ability-to-pay, opportunity to be heard, inquiry into financial status) before incarcerating for civil contempt | 448 |
| *Tkachik v Mandeville*, 487 Mich 38 (2010) | Ability to comply is an element of civil contempt in Michigan; inability is a complete defense | 45 |
| *In re Contempt of Henry*, 282 Mich App 656 (2009) | Procedural requirements for contempt in family court context | [UNVERIFIED — verify pinpoint] |

### Civil vs. Criminal Contempt Distinction

| Feature | Civil Contempt | Criminal Contempt |
|---|---|---|
| Purpose | Coercive — compel compliance | Punitive — punish past disobedience |
| Standard of proof | Clear and convincing evidence | Beyond reasonable doubt |
| Right to jury | No (generally) | Yes if incarceration >6 months (*Bloom v Illinois*, 391 US 194 (1968)) |
| Right to counsel | *Turner v Rogers*: not automatic if state provides alternatives | Yes |
| Michigan rule | MCR 3.208 (domestic relations contempt) | MCR 3.606 (criminal contempt) |

### Purge Conditions

- Purge conditions must be within the contemnor's **present** ability to perform.
- If compliance is factually impossible at time of hearing → purge is punitive in effect → must be treated as criminal contempt with full criminal protections.
- Moving party bears initial burden to show violation; burden then shifts to respondent to show inability.

### Procedural Defects Checklist

- [ ] **Muting during hearing** — denial of right to be heard violates due process
- [ ] **Remote hearing without consent** — MCR 2.407 governs; physical presence for contempt hearing may be required
- [ ] **No inquiry into financial status** — *Turner v Rogers* — required before civil contempt incarceration
- [ ] **Ambiguous or vague order** — contempt requires violation of a clear, specific order
- [ ] **No written findings** — court must make findings on ability to pay
- [ ] **Good faith efforts** — partial compliance showing good faith may negate contempt

### Defense Checklist (Five-Part)

1. **Inability:** Present financial/physical inability to comply — *Tkachik*, MCR 3.208
2. **Ambiguous order:** No willful violation of a clear order
3. **Good faith efforts:** Documented attempts at compliance
4. **Procedural defects:** Notice, right to be heard, *Turner v Rogers* requirements
5. **Denial of right to be heard:** Muting, remote hearing issues, failure to allow cross-examination

### Brief Components — MCR 7.212(C)

| Requirement | Rule | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Title page | MCR 7.212(B) | Caption, COA docket number, lower court number, parties, attorneys/pro se contact, oral argument designation |
| Table of contents | MCR 7.212(C)(1) | With page references for each section |
| Index of authorities | MCR 7.212(C)(2) | Cases, statutes, rules — alphabetical with page references |
| Jurisdictional statement | MCR 7.212(C)(3) | Basis for COA jurisdiction (MCR 7.203); lower court; date of order appealed |
| Statement of questions presented | MCR 7.212(C)(4) | Each issue phrased as a question; answered "yes" or "no" |
| Statement of facts | MCR 7.212(C)(5) | Narrative with **record citations** (e.g., "Tr. II, p 45") |
| Argument | MCR 7.212(C)(6) | Standard of review stated for each issue; legal argument with authority |
| Relief requested | MCR 7.212(C)(7) | Specific relief — reverse, remand, modify |
| Signature block | MCR 7.212(C)(8) | Attorney/pro se signature, address, bar number (if attorney) |

### Appendix Requirements — MCR 7.212(D)

- Register of actions (docket)
- Final order(s) appealed
- Any interlocutory order relevant to issues on appeal
- Transcript portions cited in brief
- Essential documents (pleadings, exhibits) referenced in argument

### Word Limit & Certificate of Compliance

- **Appellant/Appellee brief:** 16,000 words maximum — MCR 7.212(E)
- **Reply brief:** 3,500 words maximum — MCR 7.212(G)
- Certificate of compliance required: "This brief … contains [X] words" — must be signed
- Count footnotes; exclude table of contents, index of authorities, appendix, and certificate of compliance

### TrueFiling Requirements

- PDF format; OCR text layer (searchable) required
- Bookmarks for major sections required
- File size: TrueFiling limits apply (generally 50MB per document)
- File naming convention: [COA docket]-[appellant/appellee]-brief.pdf (recommended)

### Certificate of Service — MCR 7.212(G)

- Required with every brief
- Must state date and method of service on all parties
- Include service on lower court clerk if required

### Common Defects That Cause Stricken Briefs

- [ ] Missing index of authorities
- [ ] No jurisdictional statement
- [ ] Facts section without record citations
- [ ] Standard of review omitted from argument section
- [ ] Over word limit without leave
- [ ] Appendix missing register of actions or order appealed
- [ ] Certificate of compliance absent or unsigned
- [ ] TrueFiling submission not searchable (scanned image only)

## 47. COA Motions Practice During Pending Appeal

### General Motions Framework — MCR 7.211

| Motion Type | Rule | Key Requirements |
|---|---|---|
| General motions | MCR 7.211 | Filed in COA; form per MCR 7.211(C) |
| Motion to extend time | MCR 7.211(C)(8) | Good cause; not liberally granted for transcript delays |
| Motion to remand | MCR 7.211(C)(5) | Specific factual basis; legal insufficiency must appear from record |
| Emergency motion | MCR 7.211(C)(6) | Irreparable harm; supporting affidavit; same-day service on all parties |
| Peremptory disposition | MCR 7.211(C)(3) | Clear legal error; no need for full briefing |
| Motion to stay | MCR 7.209 | Four-factor test (irreparable harm, likelihood of success, balance of harms, public interest) |

### Motion to Expand Record — MCR 7.210(A)(3)

- New evidence may be added to record only by motion showing: (1) evidence not available at time of lower court proceedings; (2) relevant to issues on appeal; (3) no prejudice to opposing party.
- Courts rarely grant; expansion disfavored — preserve all evidence at trial level.

### Transcript Substitute — MCR 7.210(B)(3)

- If transcript unavailable (court reporter deceased, recording destroyed, etc.): parties may stipulate to statement of facts or settle on appeal.
- Motion required; lower court must approve agreed statement.

### Emergency Stay Pending Appeal — MCR 7.209(C)

| Element | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Irreparable harm | Specific, documented harm if stay not granted |
| Likelihood of success on merits | Meritorious legal issue — not frivolous |
| Balance of harms | Harm to movant outweighs harm to respondent |
| Public interest | Stay serves or does not harm public interest |
| Supporting affidavit | Required for emergency; must be sworn |
| Same-day service | Required for emergency motions |

### TrueFiling Emergency Procedure

- Upload motion and affidavit through TrueFiling; select "Emergency" filing type
- Call COA clerk's office to confirm receipt and processing
- Serve all parties by fastest available means (email/fax with follow-up mail)

## 48. Michigan Civil Conspiracy

### Elements

> *Authority:* *Early Detection Center v New York Life Ins Co*, 157 Mich App 618, 631 (1986) — elements of civil conspiracy: (1) a concerted action (2) by two or more persons (3) to accomplish an unlawful purpose or a lawful purpose by unlawful means (4) causing actual damages to plaintiff.

| Element | Description |
|---|---|
| (1) Concerted action | Agreement — express or implied — between two or more persons |
| (2) Two or more persons | Cannot conspire with oneself; co-conspirators need not be named defendants |
| (3) Unlawful purpose/means | Underlying tortious conduct required |
| (4) Actual damages | Pecuniary loss or injury in fact |

### Not an Independent Cause of Action

> *Authority:* *Admiral Ins Co v Columbia Casualty Ins Co*, 194 Mich App 300, 313 (1992) — civil conspiracy is not itself a cause of action independent of the underlying tort; it only enlarges the class of persons liable for the underlying tort.

- Must plead and prove an underlying tort (fraud, breach of fiduciary duty, malicious prosecution, etc.)
- Conspiracy theory allows recovery from all conspirators even if only one directly committed the tort

### State-Actor Conspiracy — 42 USC § 1983

> *Authority:* *Dennis v Sparks*, 449 US 24, 27–28 (1980) — private parties who conspire with state officials to deprive constitutional rights act "under color of law" under § 1983.

- Private actor (e.g., opposing party, GAL, private attorney) + state actor (judge, FOC officer) conspiracy → private actor liable under § 1983
- Must plead specific facts showing agreement; conclusory allegations insufficient under *Twombly*/*Iqbal*

### 42 USC § 1985(3) — Federal Conspiracy to Deprive Equal Protection

- Elements: (1) conspiracy; (2) purpose to deprive, directly or indirectly, any person of equal protection; (3) act in furtherance; (4) injury
- Class-based animus required: *Griffin v Breckenridge*, 403 US 88 (1971)
- Gender, race, national origin satisfy class requirement

### Statute of Limitations

- Michigan 3-year SOL: MCL 600.5805(2) (personal injury/tort claims)
- Accrual: date of last overt act in furtherance of conspiracy

### Pleading Requirements

- Specific facts showing: who agreed to what, when, how
- "Meeting of the minds" cannot be conclusory — plead communications, parallel conduct, motive
- Each conspirator need not know full scope; knowledge of unlawful objective sufficient

## 49. Judicial Immunity & Exceptions for § 1983

### Absolute Judicial Immunity — General Rule

> *Authority:* *Stump v Sparkman*, 435 US 349, 356–57 (1978) — judges are absolutely immune from civil liability for acts performed in their judicial capacity, even if performed maliciously or in excess of authority.

- Immunity is **absolute**, not qualified; attaches to judicial functions regardless of motive
- Applies to: Michigan circuit/district court judges, COA judges, MSC justices, probate judges, district judges

### Two Exceptions — When Immunity Does NOT Apply

| Exception | Authority | Standard |
|---|---|---|
| Non-judicial acts | *Mireles v Waco*, 502 US 9, 11–12 (1991) | Acts not taken in judge's judicial capacity (e.g., ordering clerk to act, administrative acts) |
| Clear absence of all jurisdiction | *Mireles v Waco*, 502 US 9, 11–12 (1991) | Judge has no jurisdiction whatsoever over subject matter; mere excess of jurisdiction still immune |

### Injunctive Relief — Exception to Immunity

> *Authority:* *Pulliam v Allen*, 466 US 522 (1984) — injunctive relief is available against judges acting in excess of authority; judicial immunity does not bar prospective injunctive relief.

- *Note:* 42 USC § 1983 as amended (1996) limits injunctive relief against judicial officers in § 1983 actions to cases where a declaratory decree was violated or declaratory relief is unavailable.
- Strategy: seek declaratory judgment first; if violated, then injunctive relief available.

### Court Staff & Related Actors

| Actor | Immunity | Authority |
|---|---|---|
| Law clerks | Qualified immunity | *Harlow v Fitzgerald*, 457 US 800 (1982) |
| Court clerks | Quasi-judicial immunity for court-ordered acts | *Cleavinger v Saxner*, 474 US 193 (1985) |
| Prosecutors | Absolute immunity for prosecutorial functions | *Imbler v Pachtman*, 424 US 409 (1976) |
| Prosecutors — investigative acts | Qualified immunity only | *Buckley v Fitzsimmons*, 509 US 259 (1993) |
| County/municipality | No immunity — liable for official policy/custom | *Monell v Dep't of Social Services*, 436 US 658 (1978) |

### Monell Municipal Liability

> *Authority:* *Monell v Dep't of Social Services*, 436 US 658, 690–91 (1978) — municipality liable under § 1983 only for constitutional violations resulting from: (1) official policy; (2) widespread custom; (3) failure to train amounting to deliberate indifference; (4) ratification of subordinate's unconstitutional act.

### Litigation Strategy When Judicial Immunity Bars Damages

1. Target **injunctive/declaratory relief** rather than damages against judge
2. Name the **county** as defendant under *Monell* (policy/custom, failure to train)
3. Name **non-judicial actors** (GAL, FOC officer, court-appointed evaluator) who have only qualified immunity
4. Plead § 1985(3) conspiracy if private parties conspired with state actors
5. File JTC complaint concurrently (separate administrative avenue)

## 50. Vexatious Litigant & Filing Bond Challenges

### Michigan Statutory Landscape

- Michigan has **NO** comprehensive vexatious litigant statute comparable to Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 391 et seq. [UNVERIFIED — confirm no MCL provision creating general filing bond requirement]
- Available sanctions mechanisms:

| Authority | Mechanism | Standard |
|---|---|---|
| MCL 600.2591 | Frivolous claims/defenses — attorney fees and costs | No reasonable basis in fact or law; purpose to harass |
| MCR 1.109(E) | Sanctions for filing documents not warranted by law/fact | Good-faith certification required for all filings |
| MCR 2.625 | Costs | Prevailing party entitled to taxable costs |
| MCR 7.216(C) | Vexatious appellate proceedings — damages, attorney fees | COA discretion on appeal |

### Constitutional Problems with Filing Restrictions

- *Boddie v Connecticut*, 401 US 371 (1971) — financial barriers cannot deny access to courts for fundamental rights matters
- Mich Const 1963, art I, § 13 — every person has right to appear and defend in person
- First Amendment — right to petition government for redress of grievances (*BE & K Construction Co v NLRB*, 536 US 516 (2002))
- *In forma pauperis* rights: MCR 2.002; 28 USC 1915

### Challenge Framework — Motion to Vacate Filing Bond/Prefiling Requirement

1. **No statutory basis:** Michigan courts have no general authority to impose prefiling requirements absent specific statute or court rule
2. **Constitutional access:** *Boddie* — fundamental rights (parenting/family) cannot be conditioned on payment
3. **Indigency:** MCR 2.002 — fee waiver must be available if party cannot pay
4. **Fundamental rights implicated:** Parenting time, custody = constitutional liberty interest
5. **No frivolousness finding:** MCL 600.2591 requires specific finding of frivolousness on record

### Appellate Relief Routes

| Route | Rule | Standard |
|---|---|---|
| Interlocutory appeal of order imposing bond | MCR 7.202(6)(a)(iv) | Affects substantial right |
| Application for leave | MCR 7.205 | Within 21 days of order |
| Emergency motion to stay bond order | MCR 7.209(C) | Irreparable harm — loss of access to court |

## 51. Emergency Motions — Comprehensive Procedure

### State Court Emergency Standards

| Context | Rule | Key Requirements |
|---|---|---|
| Ex parte order (general) | MCR 2.119(F) | Verified motion; show irreparable harm before notice; propose order |
| Emergency domestic relations order | MCR 3.207(B) | Affidavit showing immediate/irreparable harm; 14-day return hearing required |
| COA emergency motion | MCR 7.211(C)(6) | Irreparable harm; supporting affidavit; same-day service on all parties |
| Emergency stay pending appeal | MCR 7.209(C) | Four-factor test (see § 47); sworn affidavit |

### MCR 3.207(B) — Domestic Relations Emergency Orders

- Grounds: immediate physical danger to child or party; removal of child from jurisdiction; waste/dissipation of marital assets
- **Affidavit required:** Sworn statement of specific facts — dates, witnesses, harm
- **14-day return hearing:** Court must schedule hearing within 14 days; ex parte order expires if no hearing held
- Service: opposing party must be served with ex parte order and notice of return hearing

### Required Elements for Any Emergency Motion

- [ ] **Verified/sworn motion** (or attached affidavit) — specific facts, not conclusions
- [ ] **Nature and imminence of harm** — describe irreparable harm with specificity
- [ ] **Why no notice given** (if ex parte) — specific reason notice not feasible
- [ ] **Proposed order** — attach draft order
- [ ] **Certificate of service** — or explanation why prior service not possible
- [ ] **Contact information** — for opposing party/counsel for expedited notice

### TrueFiling Emergency Procedure

1. Upload motion + affidavit; select "Emergency" document type in TrueFiling
2. Call COA clerk's office to verbally confirm receipt and request same-day processing
3. Serve opposing parties by email or fax **simultaneously** with filing
4. Follow up with regular mail service
5. File proof of service promptly

## 52. 42 USC § 1983 Pre-Filing & Michigan-Specific Practice

### Foundational Rules

| Rule | Authority | Pinpoint |
|---|---|---|
| No exhaustion required | *Patsy v Board of Regents*, 457 US 496 (1982) | 516 (§ 1983 does not require exhaustion of state remedies) |
| Statute of limitations | MCL 600.5805(2) (Michigan 3-year personal injury SOL borrowed for § 1983) | Three years from accrual |
| SOL accrual | Federal law governs accrual — *Wallace v Kato*, 549 US 384 (2007) | Accrual when plaintiff knows or should know of injury and its cause |
| Municipal liability | *Monell v Dep't of Social Services*, 436 US 658 (1978) | Policy, custom, deliberate indifference to training |
| Qualified immunity | *Harlow v Fitzgerald*, 457 US 800 (1982) | Objective reasonableness — two-step: (1) constitutional violation; (2) clearly established law |

### Domestic Relations Exception — Does NOT Bar § 1983

> *Authority:* *Ankenbrandt v Richards*, 504 US 689 (1992) — domestic relations exception to federal jurisdiction (issuance of divorce/custody/support decrees) does **not** bar § 1983 claims challenging unconstitutional state court procedures; federal court retains jurisdiction over constitutional violations arising in domestic relations context.

### Pre-Filing Checklist — W.D. Michigan / E.D. Michigan

- [ ] Identify constitutional right violated (14th Amendment due process/equal protection; 1st, 4th Amendment as applicable)
- [ ] Identify state actor (judge — note immunity; FOC officer; county; court-appointed personnel)
- [ ] Determine *Monell* theory for county defendant (policy, custom, failure to train)
- [ ] Assess qualified immunity — is right clearly established?
- [ ] Calculate SOL — three years from date of injury (check tolling)
- [ ] Prepare civil cover sheet (JS 44) and summons
- [ ] Pay $405 filing fee or file IFP under 28 USC 1915
- [ ] Review Fed. R. Civ. P. 4 service requirements
- [ ] Plead under *Twombly*/*Iqbal* plausibility standard — specific facts, not conclusions

### Attorney Fees

> *Authority:* 42 USC § 1988 — prevailing party may recover reasonable attorney fees.
> *Kay v Ehrler*, 499 US 432 (1991) — pro se **non-lawyer** plaintiffs cannot recover attorney fees under § 1988, even if they prevail.

### Supplemental State Claims

- 28 USC § 1367 supplemental jurisdiction: federal court may hear related Michigan state law claims (tortious interference, civil conspiracy, intentional infliction of emotional distress) arising from same transaction as § 1983 claim.

### Pleading Requirements Under *Twombly*/*Iqbal*

- *Bell Atlantic Corp v Twombly*, 550 US 544 (2007) — complaint must allege enough facts to state claim to relief that is **plausible on its face**
- *Ashcroft v Iqbal*, 556 US 662 (2009) — court not required to accept conclusory allegations; factual content must allow inference of liability
- For § 1983: name each defendant; describe each defendant's specific act; identify constitutional right violated; connect act to violation

- FOIA request: MCL 15.231 et seq. — public employment records subject to disclosure unless specific exemption applies.

## 54. MCR 8.119 — Court Records & Minor Identity Confidentiality

### Protecting Minor PII in Court Filings

> *Authority:* MCR 8.119(H) — in cases involving minor children, parties must use initials (not full names) for minors in all public filings; court records identifying minors are protected.

| Rule | Requirement |
|---|---|
| MCR 8.119(H) | Use minor's initials (not full name) in all court filings open to public |
| MCR 8.119(H)(3) | Party responsible for ensuring redaction before filing |
| MCR 8.119(I) | Motion to seal records — good cause balancing test |

### Sealing Records — MCR 8.119(I)

- Grounds: protection of minor, trade secrets, ongoing investigation, specific statutory authority
- **Good cause balancing test:** privacy interest vs. public's right of access
- Court must make findings on record; blanket sealing orders disfavored
- Procedure: motion, notice to parties, opportunity to be heard

### Federal Equivalent

> *Authority:* Fed. R. Civ. P. 5.2 — in federal filings, minor's name must be reduced to initials; Social Security numbers to last four digits; dates of birth to year only; financial account numbers to last four digits.

### Practical Checklist

- [ ] Use minor's initials (e.g., "A.P.") in all publicly filed documents
- [ ] Redact Social Security numbers, dates of birth, financial account numbers
- [ ] Move to seal sensitive reports (psychological evaluations, GAL reports) if not already sealed by court
- [ ] Request that court issue blanket protective order for minor's identifying information

## 56. Landlord-Tenant — Expanded Defenses

### Retaliatory Eviction

> *Authority:* MCL 600.5720 — landlord may not terminate tenancy or bring eviction action in retaliation for tenant's good-faith assertion of legal rights (complaining to government agency, exercising legal right).

- Defense: show landlord's eviction notice followed protected activity within 90 days (prima facie retaliation)
- Burden shifts to landlord to show legitimate non-retaliatory reason
- Damages: actual damages + punitive damages up to $200 (MCL 600.5720)

### Wrongful Eviction — Treble Damages

> *Authority:* MCL 600.2918 — person wrongfully evicted or excluded from property may recover treble damages; tenant improperly removed or locked out without court process may recover three times actual damages plus attorney fees.

### Implied Warranty of Habitability

> *Authority:* *Rome v Walker*, 38 Mich App 458, 461–462 (1972) — Michigan recognizes implied warranty of habitability in residential leases; landlord must maintain premises in livable condition.

- Tenant remedies: rent withholding, repair-and-deduct, termination of lease, suit for damages
- Minimum housing standards: MCL 125.534–535; local housing codes

### Fair Housing Act — Federal Protection

> *Authority:* 42 USC 3601 et seq. — Fair Housing Act prohibits discrimination in sale/rental of housing based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, familial status, disability.

- Private right of action: 42 USC 3613
- Exhaustion optional; direct suit in federal court within 2 years
- HUD complaint: alternative to litigation

### Elliott-Larsen Civil Rights Act — Michigan Housing Protection

> *Authority:* MCL 37.2502 — Elliott-Larsen Civil Rights Act prohibits housing discrimination based on religion, race, color, national origin, age, sex, familial status, marital status, height, weight, disability.

| Protected Class (MCL 37.2502) | Additional Michigan Protection vs. Federal |
|---|---|
| Marital status | Additional |
| Age | Additional |
| Height/Weight | Additional |

- MDCR complaint: within 180 days of discriminatory act; MCL 37.2606
- Civil suit: within 3 years; MCL 37.2801

## 57. Mandamus & Superintending Control — Michigan Practice

### Constitutional Authority

> *Authority:* Mich Const 1963, art VI, § 4 — MSC has superintending control over all courts and may issue writs of mandamus, certiorari, habeas corpus, and other extraordinary writs.

### Mandamus — MCR 3.302

| Element | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Clear legal right | Petitioner must have clear legal right to performance of the act |
| Clear legal duty | Respondent must have clear legal duty to perform the ministerial act |
| Ministerial act | Act must be ministerial, not discretionary |
| No other adequate remedy | Ordinary appellate review is not adequate remedy |

> *Authority:* *Bowie v Arder*, 441 Mich 23, 36 (1992) — mandamus requires showing: (1) clear legal right to performance; (2) clear legal duty of respondent; (3) duty is ministerial; (4) no other adequate remedy in law.

### Superintending Control — MCR 7.305(B)

- **Standard:** Significant public interest; inadequate normal appellate process; necessary intervention by MSC
- **Form:** Application for superintending control — verified complaint; specific facts showing duty violated; specific relief requested
- **When to use:** Lower court refuses to perform mandatory act (hold hearing, make findings, comply with MCR); ordinary appeal insufficient

### Family Court Applications

| Situation | Mandatory Duty | Authority |
|---|---|---|
| Failure to hold required hearing | MCR 3.210 — court must hold hearing | Mandamus to compel hearing |
| Failure to make best-interest findings | MCL 722.23 — written findings required on custody change | Mandamus to compel findings |
| Failure to follow emergency order procedure | MCR 3.207(B) — 14-day return hearing required | Mandamus/superintending control |
| Refusal to rule on pending motion | No specific rule — unreasonable delay = legal duty | Mandamus to compel ruling |

### Practical Procedure — COA Mandamus

1. File **verified complaint** in COA — MCR 7.206
2. Pay filing fee or file IFP
3. Serve respondent judge (personally, through clerk of the court) and opposing parties
4. Motion for immediate consideration if emergency
5. COA may: (a) deny; (b) order respondent to show cause; (c) issue peremptory writ

## 58. JTC Complaint Practice — Step-by-Step

### Authority & Jurisdiction

> *Authority:* Mich Const 1963, art VI, § 30; MCR 9.200–9.252 — Judicial Tenure Commission (JTC) has authority to investigate and recommend discipline of Michigan judges.

| Disciplinary Action | Authority |
|---|---|
| Censure | Mich Const art VI, § 30(2) |
| Suspension (with/without pay) | Mich Const art VI, § 30(2) |
| Removal from office | Mich Const art VI, § 30(2) |
| Involuntary retirement | Mich Const art VI, § 30(2) |

### Who May File

- Any person — no requirement of party status; attorneys, litigants, witnesses, and members of the public may file

### Grounds for Complaint

> *Authority:* Mich Const 1963, art VI, § 30(2) — judicial misconduct: (a) persistent failure to perform judicial duties; (b) habitual intemperance; (c) conduct clearly prejudicial to the administration of justice; (d) conviction of a felony; (e) conduct inconsistent with office.

### Filing Procedure

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Complete JTC Complaint Form (JTC-1) — available at jtc.courts.mi.gov |
| 2 | Provide: judge's name/court, specific facts, dates, supporting documents (transcripts, orders) |
| 3 | Mail to: Judicial Tenure Commission, 3034 W Grand Blvd., Suite 8-450, Detroit, MI 48202 |
| 4 | No filing fee required |
| 5 | Retain copy of all submitted materials |

### Confidentiality

> *Authority:* MCR 9.221 — JTC complaint proceedings are **confidential** until formal complaint is filed by JTC and served on respondent judge; disclosure of proceedings prior to that point may be contempt of court.

- Cannot publicly disclose that JTC complaint was filed until JTC formally charges judge
- After formal complaint filed: proceedings become public

### Timeline [UNVERIFIED]

- Initial review: typically 30–90 days
- Investigation (if opened): typically 3–18 months depending on complexity
- Formal complaint → public hearing → MSC decision

### Critical Limitation

> **Does NOT automatically disqualify judge.** JTC complaint does not disqualify judge from presiding over your case. A **separate MCR 2.003 motion** for disqualification must be filed in the trial court.

## 59. Disqualification Post-Denial Strategy

### Step 1 — Motion for Review with Chief Judge

> *Authority:* MCR 2.003(D)(3) — if assigned judge denies disqualification, party may file motion for review by chief judge of the court within 21 days.

- Chief judge must review motion de novo
- If chief judge is the assigned judge or is conflicted: disclose conflict; request assignment to another chief judge or random assignment of substitute

### Step 2 — Interlocutory Appeal to COA

> *Authority:* MCR 7.202(6)(a)(iv) — order affecting substantial right is appealable by right if it effectively determines action; disqualification denial affecting fundamental constitutional right qualifies.

| Route | Rule | Deadline |
|---|---|---|
| Claim of appeal (if right) | MCR 7.203(A)(1) | 21 days from entry of final order or earlier if substantial right |
| Application for leave | MCR 7.205 | 21 days from entry of order denying disqualification |
| Emergency motion to stay | MCR 7.209(C) | Simultaneously with leave application |

### Step 3 — Preserve Record of Bias

- [ ] Objection on the record at every hearing where biased conduct occurs
- [ ] Supplement disqualification motion with specific incidents from transcript
- [ ] File supplemental motions for disqualification after each new biased act (each act is a new ground)
- [ ] Order transcripts of all hearings immediately — preserve before destruction

### Step 4 — MSC Superintending Control

> *Authority:* MCR 7.305(B) — application for superintending control to MSC; significant public interest; inadequate ordinary appellate process.

### Waiver Risk

> *Authority:* *Cain v Dep't of Corrections*, 451 Mich 470, 497 (1996) — party who fails to timely raise disqualification issue waives it; must raise at first opportunity.

- File disqualification motion **at first opportunity** — as soon as grounds are known
- Participating in proceedings without objection may waive right to disqualification

### JTC Complaint (Parallel Track)

- File JTC complaint concurrently — does not substitute for MCR 2.003 motion but creates parallel administrative record
- JTC investigation may produce findings useful in subsequent § 1983 action

## 60. Damages Calculation — Family Law & § 1983

### Constitutional Damages — Federal Framework

| Category | Authority | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Nominal damages | *Carey v Piphus*, 435 US 247, 266–267 (1978) | Available for proven constitutional violation even without proof of actual injury; at least $1 |
| Compensatory damages | *Memphis Community School Dist v Stachura*, 477 US 299, 307 (1986) | Must be tied to actual injury; no "abstract value" of constitutional rights |
| Punitive damages | *Smith v Wade*, 461 US 30, 56 (1983) | Available against individual defendant who acted with reckless or callous indifference to federally protected rights |
| Emotional distress | *Carey v Piphus* | Compensable under § 1983 with specific proof — testimony, treatment records, impact on daily life |

### Five Categories of Damages

| Category | Description | Proof |
|---|---|---|
| (1) Compensatory | Actual loss caused by constitutional violation | Medical/therapy bills, school records, expert testimony |
| (2) Economic | Lost wages, increased housing costs, legal fees expended | Pay stubs, invoices, tax returns |
| (3) Punitive | Reckless/callous indifference by individual defendants | *Smith v Wade* — subjective recklessness shown by conduct |
| (4) Nominal | Proven violation, no proven injury | $1 minimum; available as matter of law |
| (5) Injunctive | Future compliance; modification of policy | Ongoing irreparable harm; no adequate remedy at law |

### Attorney Fees (§ 1983)

> *Authority:* 42 USC § 1988 — prevailing party in § 1983 action may recover reasonable attorney fees from losing party.
> *Kay v Ehrler*, 499 US 432, 435–436 (1991) — **pro se non-lawyers** cannot recover attorney fees under § 1988, even if they prevail.

### Michigan Family Law Damages

| Authority | Remedy | Standard |
|---|---|---|
| MCL 722.27b | Makeup parenting time + attorney fees for wrongful denial | Parent wrongfully denied court-ordered parenting time |
| MCL 552.641 | Contempt — court may order payment of costs, expenses, attorney fees | Other party incurred costs due to contempt |
| MCL 600.2591 | Frivolous claim costs — attorney fees and actual costs | Prevailing party if opposing party's claims/defenses frivolous |

### Expert Testimony — Damages in Parenting Cases

| Expert | Subject Matter | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Child psychologist/therapist | Parent-child attachment, psychological harm from severance | Quantify emotional and developmental harm to child |
| Child development expert | Long-term developmental impact of parental deprivation | Establish causation and damages for extended separation |
| Economic expert | Lost wages, career disruption due to litigation | Economic damages component |
| Medical expert | Physical/psychological harm to parent | Emotional distress + physical sequelae |

### Damages Calculation Checklist

- [ ] Document each specific constitutional violation with date, actor, and harm
- [ ] Calculate total parenting time lost (hours × reasonable compensation for lost parent-child relationship)
- [ ] Compile all legal expenses actually incurred (invoices, receipts)
- [ ] Document economic losses (missed work, job loss, relocation costs)
- [ ] Obtain medical/therapy records documenting treatment for emotional distress
- [ ] Identify punitive damages targets — individual defendants with reckless/callous conduct
- [ ] Retain expert witness for child-attachment harm if long-term separation
